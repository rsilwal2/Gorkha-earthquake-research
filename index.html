Studying Co-Seismic Deformation of the 2015 Gorkha Earthquake Using Radar Remote Sensing

Mapping Regional Surface Displacement Patterns Using ALOS-2 ScanSAR InSAR Data

Rajesh Silwal

Explore Research

Introduction

The 2015 Gorkha Earthquake (Mw 7.8) and its largest aftershock (Mw 7.3) generated extensive surface deformation along a ~200 km stretch of the Main Himalayan Thrust in central Nepal. In this project, I produced a regional-scale coseismic displacement map using ALOS-2 ScanSAR data processed with interferometric synthetic aperture radar (InSAR). I mosaicked five descending subswaths to create a ~350 km-wide scene that fully captures the deformation footprints of both the mainshock and the Kodari aftershock. I processed the data using both ISCE2 and GMTSAR workflows, taking advantage of L-band‚Äôs longer wavelength to preserve interferometric coherence over the heavily vegetated and rugged Himalayan topography. To mitigate ionospheric disturbances typical of wide-swath L-band data, I tested and compared split-spectrum and polynomial trend-fitting methods, ultimately selecting the approach that yielded the cleanest and most accurate displacement field. In addition to deformation mapping, I analyzed the spatial relationship between coseismic ground displacement and thousands of earthquake-triggered landslides.

These results play a key role in enhancing the understanding of surface deformation caused by earthquake rupture along the Main Himalayan Thrust, improving regional seismic hazard assessment, and clarifying the link between strong coseismic deformation and landslide triggering.

Conclusions

L-Band Radar in Vegetated Terrain:

  • L-band radar is essential for accurate deformation mapping in vegetated regions like Nepal.
  • Its penetration capability enables reliable measurements despite dense vegetation cover.
  • L-band coherence preservation reduces errors and improves deformation accuracy.

ALOS-2 ScanSAR Coverage for Regional Mapping:

  • Wide-swath acquisition effectively captures complete regional deformation patterns.
  • Seamless coverage eliminates data gaps and boundary artifacts in rupture analysis.
  • Single-pass imaging improves efficiency in post-disaster deformation mapping.
  • ALOS-2 ScanSAR is critical for comprehensive understanding of earthquake-induced crustal displacement.

Ionosphere Correction for Accuracy:

  • Ionospheric correction is necessary to distinguish true deformation from atmospheric artifacts.
  • Without correction, significant phase delays can produce false deformation signals.
  • Corrected interferograms provide reliable basis for rupture characterization and hazard assessment.
